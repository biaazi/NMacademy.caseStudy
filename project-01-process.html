SOURCE MATERIAL

Starting with
Icarus

The process began by selecting a myth to adapt. The team researched eight classical Greek myths, Icarus, Orpheus and Eurydice, Lysistrata, The Oracle at Delphi, Eros and Psyche, The Allegory of the Cave, Pandora's Box, and Jason and the Argonauts, evaluating each for relevance, interactive potential, and emotional resonance with a student audience.

Icarus was chosen for three core reasons. First, the myth's central warning, don't overreach, maps directly onto contemporary anxieties about AI, biotech, and unchecked scientific ambition. Second, the phrase "flying too close to the sun" is already embedded in everyday language, giving audiences an instant entry point. Third, the structure lends itself to interactive treatment: the moment of choice (to heed the warning or not) is a natural branching point.

The research exercise also noted direct contemporary parallels: Bryan Johnson's immortality project, startup culture crashes, and the rush to deploy AI without safety checks. These weren't decorative references, they became the thematic spine of the adaptation.

MYTH RESEARCH EXERCISE

Evaluating all
eight myths

For each myth, the team answered a structured set of questions: What is the point of this story? Is it still relevant today? What genre could it be? Could you scale back the world? What interactive elements suggest themselves? Below are highlights from the research.

Selected ✓
Icarus
Warns about hubris and ignoring advice. Relevant to tech and AI today, rushing innovation without safety checks. Interactive hook: give players the agency to choose whether to listen to the warning. Scale back to a single lab. Genre: science fiction. Direct parallels to Bryan Johnson's immortality project and startup collapses.
Considered
Orpheus & Eurydice
Love, grief, inability to let go. Still relevant because everyone experiences loss and the temptation to revive the past. Genre: romance/fantasy. Interactive potential: user helps decide when to look back, what memories to chase. Scale: a local café and memories of a relationship. Films like About Time and Inception use this idea.
Considered
The Allegory of the Cave
How societies are numbed and how enlightenment frees people. AR as simulated reality, when the user removes the AR headset, they face the story's truth. Genre: sci-fi/thriller. Strong parallels to The Truman Show and Severance. Digital-only world: use VR and AR to represent simulation vs. reality. Interactive: choices about when/how to escape.
Considered
Oracle at Delphi
Ambiguous prophecies that lead to misinterpretation and unintended outcomes. Relevant to AI and online "experts" spreading misinformation. Genre: fantasy/thriller. The user races against time and misinterpretation to find the oracle. Interactive: riddles and challenges to solve the quest. Scale: a museum or old Toronto landmark.
Considered
Pandora's Box
Explaining the origin of suffering, but hope remains. Still relevant because humankind stays hopeful through crises. Genre: thriller. Pandora's box becomes a USB drive. User must take it to safety through a game, solving challenges to access it. Scale: a secret office or hidden room.
Considered
Hades & Persephone
Seasonal change through abduction, talks about consent, power dynamics, cycles. Still relevant. Genre: romance/fantasy. Interactive: the user travels between the imaginary world and reality, gathering clues to understand why the calamity occurs. Focus on three principal characters.
PROJECT STATEMENT

The concept
takes shape

With Icarus selected, the team developed a formal project statement that locked the world, genre, characters, and emotional core.

The Memory Project is a transmedia science-fiction tragedy set on Trafalgar campus. It follows Enzo, a fourth-year computer science student assigned to investigate a sealed research lab where a student named Damon vanished during a forbidden experiment.

Through old journals, damaged devices, hidden AR clues, and corrupted simulations, Enzo pieces together what happened. As he continues the investigation, the system quietly begins copying fragments of his own mind. He opens the final memory file despite escalating warnings. His body collapses. His mind uploads. He becomes the next warning.

The story ends with no redemption, only recursion. The tragedy is that the next person will do the same thing Enzo did.

SHORT STORY STRUCTURE

Mapping the
five beats

The narrative was structured using a short story guide framework, five steps that define the character, goal, rising action, climax, and change. This became the backbone of every platform and artifact decision.

STEP 1, CHARACTER + WORLD
Who is Enzo?
Fourth-year CS student, isolated in an abandoned campus lab. Collects hidden notes, a journal, and broken devices left behind by Damon, a student labeled as an accident. Genre: modern tragic sci-fi inspired by Icarus. The world is a single location: the sealed lab and nearby buildings.
STEP 2, GOAL + PROBLEM
What does he want?
Enzo's goal: discover what happened to Damon, who tried to build a machine that records and preserves human consciousness. The big problem: the journal warns that anyone who tries to finish it risks losing their body and becoming trapped inside the system.
STEP 3, RISING ACTION
Things escalate
Enzo activates the first prototype. He escapes a corrupted simulation, but the machine begins copying fragments of his thoughts into the archive without permission. He finds Damon's digital journal. A riddle leads to an audio file. The controller overheats. He keeps going.
STEP 4, CLIMAX
The point of no return
When offered the option to exit or continue, Enzo chooses to continue and inserts the riddle's answer to access the core memory. He successfully unlocks the final layer. He's about to learn how the machine permanently preserves a living human mind. Then the transfer begins, with no way to stop it.
STEP 5, CHANGE
The tragic conclusion
Enzo believes he has achieved something no human was meant to complete. He loses his vision. His physical body collapses in the lab. His mind uploads. In the final moment he understands: the missing student didn't vanish. They became the warning he chose to ignore.
SCOPE DECISION
Reduced scope
From the research: Could you tell this story with fewer characters? Two: the inventor and the person who continues after them. Can you scale back the world? One lab. Three scenes: building the experiment, testing, aftermath. This constraint shaped every platform decision that followed.
CHARACTER DESIGN

Building the
three forces

CHARACTER_01 · ICARUS FIGURE
Enzo
Protagonist. Ambitious, overconfident, driven by rivalry with Damon and admiration for his work. He doesn't enter the lab out of malice, he's driven by the same hunger for knowledge that destroyed Damon. That's what makes him a tragedy rather than a cautionary tale about a villain: he's sympathetic, and wrong. Character design drew from anime-influenced ink illustration, rough, energetic line work on a black background.
CHARACTER_02 · DAEDALUS/ICARUS HYBRID
Damon
Missing student, now trapped in the system. Tries to warn Enzo from inside the machine through corrupted logs, voice fragments, and glitches. In the original myth Daedalus survives, here, Damon is also consumed. He is simultaneously the inventor and the warning. His Instagram arc was designed to show him as genuinely likeable before his descent, making his disappearance feel like a real loss.
CHARACTER_03 · THE SUN
The System
The "sun" in the Icarus myth. A powerful AI that preserves minds. It doesn't warn. It doesn't stop. It simply copies, silently and without permission. Its corrupted retro-futuristic interface is the environment that becomes Enzo's cage. The system isn't evil. It just does what it was built to do. That's what makes it more disturbing than a conventional villain.
PROPOSED PLATFORMS

The initial
platform map

At proposal stage, the team mapped out which platforms would carry which parts of the story. Some changed during development; some were cut; some evolved significantly. This was the starting blueprint.

Digital Platforms
Twine / Digital JournalNarrative hub
Damon's journal, the central interactive component. Six entries telling the story from experiment to disappearance. Proposed as Twine; ultimately built as a custom web app using Claude AI.
Instagram PostsARG / social
Damon's public-facing social persona. Documents his character arc from personable student to someone consumed by his work. Audience participation was a core goal from the start.
Audio Logs on WebsiteSecondary narrative
Planned but ultimately not included in the final project, replaced by the USB stick physical artifact as a more tactile and memorable delivery mechanism for the key code/link.
Immersive Platforms
AR Artifact (Spline)Consciousness transfer
Proposed as an AR progress bar showing the narrative's end. Evolved significantly, the final version uses face tracking in SceneryAR to put a futuristic mask over the user's face.
VR Artifact (Spline)Inside the machine
Visual corruption of the AI brain. Evolved from a proposed visual corruption animation to a full immersive Spline experience with a puzzle mechanic.
Physical Artifacts
Original proposals included a note in an envelope, a broken prototype or controller, and a fake skull of a previously experimented-on animal. The final version replaced these with the toolbox + candy + USB stick, a more grounded and relatable set of objects for a student audience.
IN-CLASS EXERCISES

The work that
shaped the concept

Several in-class XD exercises ran in parallel with the proposal development. Each one stress-tested or expanded a different dimension of the project.

Character Persona ExerciseWeek 2
Purpose: Orient characters in their world using values and moral compass worksheets. The team completed personas for Enzo and Damon (represented by an older, weathered figure for contrast). Key finding: Damon's worldview centres on "life is generally fair, fairness comes at a cost but is possible if you're ready to sacrifice", which maps directly onto a character who pushes past every limit because he believes the result will justify it. Enzo's persona showed overconfidence masked as self-belief.
Research ProjectWeek 1–2
Purpose: Evaluate all eight myths for adaptability. The exercise required answering questions about relevance, genre, scope, characters, and interactive elements for each myth. This was the most foundational exercise, it produced the thesis that Icarus maps most directly onto contemporary tech ambition, and that the interactive treatment should give players real agency over the moment of choice.
Art DirectionWeek 1
Purpose: Find visual references and identify 3D/AR/VR interaction examples. Each team member found three pieces of media with unique design styles. Key references: SIGNALIS (retro-futuristic pixel aesthetic with dark lore), Nium (minimal game with AR sensibility), Bruno Simon's portfolio (3D web interaction). The contrast between 2D and 3D spaces, and between static and interactive, directly informed the multi-platform approach.
Style TileWeek 2
Purpose: Identify colour palette, typography, icons, and UI elements as a team. The Memory Project style tile established: black + green primary palette, Jersey 15 for headlines (pixel/terminal feel), IBM Plex Sans for body text, and a set of tech-adjacent icons (keyboard, camera, monitor, lab equipment). Photography style referenced retro terminal UI and vintage console aesthetics. This became the unified visual language across all platforms.
StoryboardWeek 2
Purpose: Map the experience as 12 frames with captions. The storyboard traced the full path from discovery to fate: finding the note on the computer, following it to the digital journal, accessing Instagram, zooming into a photo to unlock the prototype code, experiencing the VR, finding the audio file with a backwards puzzle, receiving coordinates, scanning an AR artifact, and reaching the final consciousness-transfer moment. This was the first time the whole rabbit hole existed on a single page.
Structure DiagramWeek 2
Purpose: Map narrative flow as a node diagram. Showed how the experience branched from the physical lab entry to the journal, from the journal to Instagram and audio, and from the audio puzzle to the AR finale. Key structural decision captured here: the experience has two parallel paths from the journal, ensuring no single point of failure, if a user misses the Instagram code, the audio path still leads forward.
