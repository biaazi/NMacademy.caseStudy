FROM P2 TO P3

What changed,
what was added

Project 3 was about completion and validation. The prototypes from P2 were finished, new artifacts were added that hadn't existed in the proposal, the character arcs were locked down with greater specificity, and usability testing was conducted across every component.

The most significant additions were two new artifacts that weren't in the original proposal: the handwritten angry note as a distinct first hook, and Damon's blue USB stick as a physical-to-digital bridge between the VR and the access code website.

The VR also evolved substantially, the ESC button was replaced by a more thematically resonant scrambled image puzzle revealing the USB stick location. And the AR went from a loading bar prototype to a full face-tracking consciousness mask with a final undismissable error message.

Character arcs were also sharpened: Enzo is now explicitly framed as chasing an academic rival, adding personal motivation that makes his hubris more believable. Damon's arc culminates in his last Instagram post visually erasing him from reality.

VR, DEVELOPMENT & ITERATION

Building the
corrupted mind

The VR experience required the most iteration of any component in the project. Spline was difficult to control on mobile, the puzzle mechanic took multiple rebuilds to work correctly, and user testing revealed the camera movement was causing dizziness. Every problem was solved, but none of them easily.

The approach: find a Drag-Drop Puzzle game template on Spline that was simple enough to run on a phone. When the image didn't fit the puzzle pieces correctly, a sequence puzzle was attempted instead, but Spline's conditions and variables didn't behave as intended. The team returned to the original puzzle template and worked through its logic. Grok was used to generate a techy, neo-futuristic puzzle image, and components from the Holo-Desire Spline community project were added for the grasping hands effect.

After user testing flagged that the puzzle looked static (users didn't realise the pieces could be rearranged), Figma was used to build a puzzle cutout template and Photoshop to cut out individual pieces with proper puzzle-piece shapes. Camera animations were simplified from a complex path to a flatter experience with subtler shifts after users reported dizziness. Finally, multiple grasping hands with animated movements were added behind the puzzle to create pressure and threat.

PUZZLE TEMPLATE, DRAG DROP PUZZLE (Spline)
The starting point: 'Drag Drop Puzzle' by celest on Spline Community. Chosen because it was simple enough to run on mobile. The colourful blob image was replaced with a custom techy puzzle image, and the logic was reworked to fit the narrative.
EARLY ITERATION, ICARUS7 PUZZLE IN SPLINE
Early iteration of the adapted puzzle in Spline, showing the ICARUS7 circuit-board image applied to the puzzle pieces. The scattered scrambled pieces are visible around the central preview. At this stage the pieces were flat squares without proper puzzle-piece cutouts, which is why users couldn't tell they were rearrangeable.
PUZZLE IMAGE, ADOBE ILLUSTRATOR
Creating the final puzzle image in Adobe Illustrator, a circuit-board style composition featuring a computer workstation desk (the same desk where the USB stick is planted in the real classroom). The image was generated using Grok AI for the base and then refined in Illustrator with the circuit-trace border pattern.
HOLO-DESIRE, COMMUNITY PROJECT (Spline)
The 'Holo-Desire' project by marcelomathias on Spline Community, the source for the grasping, threatening hands that surround the puzzle. The glowing cyan wireframe hands, floating geometry, and dark atmosphere all matched the corrupted AI world. Components were extracted and adapted into the final scene.
PUZZLE TEMPLATE, FIGMA
After user testing revealed the scrambled pieces looked static and non-interactive, Figma was used to build a precise 3×3 puzzle grid template, mapping the exact dimensions and positions of the cutouts so the pieces could be cut correctly in Photoshop. The right side shows the original Drag-Drop Puzzle template image overlaid for reference.
PUZZLE PIECE MASK, PHOTOSHOP
Individual puzzle piece mask created in Photoshop, using the Figma template to cut out proper puzzle-piece shaped tiles with interlocking tabs. Each piece was exported separately and imported back into Spline, replacing the plain square tiles that had confused users in testing.
FINAL VR SCENE, 'CORRUPTED SYSTEM' IN SPLINE
The final 'Corrupted System' scene in Spline. Multiple red wireframe hands with grasping animations surround the central ICARUS7 puzzle. A central cyan hand reaches from above. The dark grid floor, particle effects, fog, and green ambient glow create the retro-futuristic corrupted AI atmosphere. The camera path (visible in the left panel) was simplified from the original complex motion after user testing flagged dizziness. Background colour: #090A20.

FINALIZED CHARACTER ARCS

Arcs
solidified

ENZO · ICARUS
The overconfident rival
P3 added a key detail to Enzo's motivation: he's not just curious, he's chasing an academic rival. His passion towards Damon, mixed with his own ambition, pushes him down a path he can't return from. This makes the hubris feel earned rather than abstract. He doesn't ignore the warnings because he's reckless, he ignores them because he believes he can do what Damon couldn't. That's the specific shape of his arrogance.
DAMON · WARNING
The erasure arc
Damon's complete arc was finalized in P3: starts personable and relatable, then becomes someone who lets his ambition concern the people around him. His final Instagram post visually appears to erase him from reality, a formal ending to his presence in the world before Enzo picks up the thread. The visual deletion is the last thing he leaves behind in public before disappearing into the system.
AI TOOLS IN P3

The expanded
AI toolkit

P3 expanded the team's use of AI tools beyond Claude, experimenting with multiple platforms for different asset types and documenting both successes and limitations honestly.

⚙️
Claude, Journal + Iteration
Continued as the primary build tool, powering journal development, rapid style iteration, content writing, and technical troubleshooting. When usability testing surfaced issues, Claude-generated fixes were applied through follow-up prompts: lock icons, font scaling, structural navigation changes. The full feedback loop from test finding to deployed fix ran faster than any traditional development process.
🎨
Gemini → Grok, Visual Assets
Gemini AI was the first choice for generating USB stick visuals. After evaluating the results, the team switched to Grok, which produced stronger tech-themed imagery. This is documented as a deliberate comparison process, not a failure of Gemini, but a recognition that different AI tools have different strengths, and testing multiple options is part of professional AI-assisted workflow.
🧊
Spline AI, 3D Exploration
Spline AI was used to experiment with generating 3D assets for the VR experience alongside manually adapted community scenes. The comparison informed decisions about where AI generation added value versus where direct adaptation was faster and more precise. For the VR in this project, manual adaptation of existing scenes was the better approach, AI-generated 3D didn't match the project's specific visual requirements.
⚠️
Claude, Spline Troubleshoot (Failed)
Claude was used to attempt to resolve a Spline VR integration issue. The brainstormed solution wasn't successfully implemented, the activity was not carried forward. This is documented deliberately, not hidden. AI tools don't always produce usable results. Understanding where the limitations are, and when to stop pursuing an AI-assisted path, is as important as knowing when to use AI in the first place.
🧠
AI, Brainstorming Throughout
AI was used as a collaborative thinking partner across the full project lifecycle, refining narrative concept, workshopping themes and artifact logic, drafting and iterating on written content, and planning the audience experience. The team treated it as a member who's always available for a brainstorm session: fast, non-judgmental, and useful for generating options to evaluate rather than decisions to accept wholesale.
📋
AI, Project Documentation
AI supported grammar, phrasing, and polishing of the formal project documentation throughout, keeping written content consistent in tone and quality across all three project submissions. This case study website itself was built using Claude, continuing the pattern established with the journal: describe the concept in natural language, iterate through follow-up prompts, deploy.
PROJECT MANAGEMENT

How the team
stayed on track

The project was divided across three defined roles, Visual Design & AR (Marion), Project Manager & Narrative Lead (Rabia), Immersive Media Lead (Alejandra), ensuring clear ownership over every task. Role clarity meant no task was ambiguously assigned, and everyone knew what they were accountable for.

A Gantt chart tracked deliverables from proposal through final delivery, mapping tasks to team members and deadlines. It was updated continuously, not just created and forgotten. Checking it weekly made delays visible before they became critical.

The narrative concept was locked early, before development began in earnest. This was a deliberate sequencing decision: you can't build consistent artifacts if the story keeps changing underneath them. Getting the characters, arc, and platforms agreed on in P1 freed P2 and P3 to focus entirely on execution.

Physical, digital, and immersive artifacts were developed in parallel, designed from the start to connect into one cohesive player journey. Feedback loops were built in, usability testing findings drove direct revisions before final delivery, not after.
