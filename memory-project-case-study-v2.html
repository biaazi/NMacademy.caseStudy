PROJECT SYNOPSIS

Where has your
mind gone?

The Memory Project is a transmedia science-fiction tragedy that reimagines the Greek myth of Icarus as a cautionary tale about unchecked ambition in the age of digital consciousness.

Set on Trafalgar campus, the story follows Enzo, a fourth-year computer science student assigned to investigate a sealed research lab after a student named Damon vanished during a forbidden experiment.

Through old journals, damaged devices, hidden AR clues, and corrupted simulations, Enzo pieces together the truth: Damon tried to upload human consciousness into a machine, and it worked. As Enzo continues the investigation, the system quietly begins copying his mind.

In the end, Enzo opens the final memory file despite escalating warnings. His body collapses. His mind uploads. He becomes the next warning.

CHARACTER DESIGN

Three forces,
one tragedy

CHARACTER_01
Enzo
Icarus figure · Protagonist
A fourth-year computer science student driven by rivalry with Damon and admiration for his work. Enzo's passion towards his classmate mixed with his own ambition pushes him down a path he can't return from. His overconfidence mirrors the mythic Icarus, he ignores every warning, and in the end, joins Damon in his fate.
CHARACTER_02
Damon
Daedalus / Icarus figure · The Warning
Starts off as a personable, relatable student who likes to work hard and push himself. But his ambition wins out over self-preservation, he becomes someone who concerns the people around him, and eventually meets his doom. His last Instagram post visually erases him from reality. Now present only as fragments: voice recordings, corrupted logs, glitches.
CHARACTER_03
The System
The Sun · The Machine / AI
The "sun" in the Icarus myth. A powerful AI that silently observes, collects, and preserves. It does not warn. It does not stop. It simply copies, first Damon, then fragments of Enzo, until the transfer is complete. Its retro-futuristic corrupted interface becomes the final environment Enzo cannot escape.
NARRATIVE STRUCTURE

The arc of
hubris

Entry Point

The Note + Toolbox

An angry handwritten note posted near classroom AA1A reads: "To whoever left a red box full of wires in AA1A, please remove them!" Designed like "office drama," it sparks curiosity and draws people into the room. Inside: Damon's red toolbox, left half-open with real jumper wires draped out. Opening it reveals Twizzler candy bags resembling wires and a QR code linking to Damon's journal.

Discovery

Damon's Digital Journal

The QR code leads to damons-end-journal.vercel.app, a CRT/terminal aesthetic journal with glitch effects and scanlines. Six entries unlock progressively, chronicling Damon's arc from first experiment to disappearance. The journal breaks the fourth wall by referencing real Sheridan classroom numbers, grounding Damon as a believable IXD student. Embedded clues link to Instagram and the VR experience.

Escalation

Instagram → VR: Into the System

Damon's Instagram (@damontorre.s) documents his full arc, humble and personable at first, then consumed by his work, his final post visually erasing him from reality. Real audience members have commented with growing concern. A hidden code in his post unlocks the Spline VR experience. Giant hands try to trap the user inside the corrupted AI mind. Strong colour shifts from blue to red signal something is deeply wrong. The only escape: solving the scrambled image puzzle to find the USB stick clue.

Physical Pivot

Damon's USB Stick

Completing the VR puzzle reveals an image of the classroom AA1A desk, and Damon's blue USB stick sitting on it. The physical USB is planted in the actual room. Plugging it in reveals one file: a text document containing a single URL.

Unlock

ICARUS7, Access Code Website

The URL leads to icarus7.vercel.app, a terminal page displaying the access code ICARUS7. Hovering triggers a red/cyan chromatic glitch; animated scanlines pass top to bottom; corner bracket markers and a pulsing live status dot complete the world. Entering this code into the journal unlocks the final chapter.

Resolution

AR Face-Scan, Consciousness Transfer Complete

The final experience at scnry.app/SQ5TS9Jti2b uses face tracking via SceneryAR. The user sees an innocuous loading symbol, then a glossy futuristic mask locks onto their face alongside the message: "SYSTEM CONSCIOUSNESS UPLOAD COMPLETE. YOU ARE NO MORE AND HAVE JOINED DAMON IN HIS FATE. ERASURE FROM REALITY IMMINENT." Neither the mask nor the message can be dismissed. Enzo joins Damon. The narrative ends.

"The missing student did not vanish, they became the warning he chose to ignore."

AI AS COLLABORATOR

AI as a
team member

⚙️
Claude, Journal Development
The entire interactive digital journal was built using Claude through direct natural language prompts, no manual coding from scratch. The team described concept, theme, and functions; Claude generated a fully functional, aesthetically consistent web application. Ongoing troubleshooting and technical iteration throughout the build were also handled via Claude. An attempt to troubleshoot Spline VR through Claude was made but ultimately not carried forward.
🔄
Claude, Rapid Iteration
Design changes that would have taken days were completed in hours. After user testing flagged issues, font too small, too much visual noise, navigation confusion, all revisions were applied instantly through follow-up Claude prompts. Lock icons, bolder fonts, stronger glow effects, and reorganized navigation were all iterated this way, compressing what would otherwise be a multi-day redesign cycle.
🧠
AI, Narrative Brainstorming
AI was used as a collaborative brainstorming tool throughout to develop, refine, and iterate on the narrative concept, themes, artifact integration logic, and audience experience design. It also assisted in drafting and workshopping written content, journal entries, artifact copy, and project documentation, keeping tone consistent across all platforms.
🎨
Gemini + Grok, Visual Assets
Gemini AI was used initially to generate visual assets for the USB stick artifact. The team later switched to Grok, which produced stronger results for tech-themed imagery. This iterative comparison of tools reflects the team's experimental approach, testing multiple AI platforms to find the best fit for specific asset types rather than defaulting to one tool for everything.
🧊
Spline AI, 3D Assets
Spline AI was experimented with for creating 3D assets within the VR experience. The team explored its capabilities alongside manually adapted Spline community scenes to build the corrupted AI mind environment. This hands-on comparison informed decisions about where AI generation added value versus where manual adaptation was faster and more controllable.
📋
Honest Limitations
Not every AI-assisted attempt succeeded. Claude was used to troubleshoot an aspect of the Spline VR experience, but the brainstormed solution wasn't successfully implemented and was not carried forward. This is documented deliberately, the team's approach to AI treats it as a collaborator with real capabilities and real limits, not a guaranteed fix.

USABILITY TESTING

What users
revealed

The Note Physical Entry Point

Users reported it was eye-catching and made them wonder who got mad and what the fuss was about. The percentage who wondered about the note was lower than those who actually went into the room, which is standard for passive entry points. The note successfully drove foot traffic to the physical artifact.

  • Effective at sparking curiosity and inciting an emotional reaction
  • Drop-off between noticing and acting is expected, not a failure
  • Two physical rabbit holes (note + toolbox) attempted to offset "QR code burnout"
The Toolbox Physical Artifact

Praised as big, red, and very easy to find once in the room. Users expressed light enjoyment at receiving the candy reward, a small but meaningful gesture that made participation feel worthwhile. Drop-off between finding the box and continuing the experience was noted as standard for opt-in interactive work.

  • High visibility, the red box was immediately apparent
  • Candy reward created a moment of delight and goodwill
  • Some "QR code burnout" noted across the overall class project display
Journal Website Digital Hub

Users were initially confused by navigation, lock icons were added to clearly signal which chapters were unavailable. Mysterious flavour text worked as intended, acting as a red herring and adding visual intrigue. The glitchy tech design was praised. Users reported strong narrative intrigue about the writing style and how it was integrated into the school's reality.

  • Added lock icons to unavailable journal entries → resolved confusion
  • Mysterious flavour text praised as effective atmospheric red herring
  • Design continuity across the full experience was noticed and appreciated
  • Users successfully recalled back to previously locked sections after completion
Instagram Social / ARG

Users continued to correctly interpret Damon's personality and character arc. They expressed genuine enthusiasm engaging creatively with his account and enjoying his journey. Two rabbit holes, one in the post, one in the comments, ensured the transition to the VR experience was clear regardless of which path a user discovered.

  • Character arc landing correctly, users perceived Damon's descent accurately
  • Audience enthusiasm confirmed the social platform was effective narrative territory
  • Dual rabbit holes (post + comments) prevent dead ends in the experience flow
VR Experience Spline / Immersive

Users felt controlled, described the sensation as feeling "like a puppet," which was the intended effect. Camera movement was flagged as too fast and caused dizziness. The floating hands did not feel threatening enough to users. Critically: users did not realize the scrambled images were an interactive puzzle, they appeared to look fixed.

  • ✓ Felt like a puppet, loss-of-control sensation worked
  • ✗ Camera speed too fast, caused dizziness, especially near the second hand
  • ✗ Floating hands not threatening, visual redesign needed
  • ✗ Puzzle interactivity unclear, scrambled images looked static, not rearrangeable
AR Experience SceneryAR / Face Tracking

Users reported the experience was scary and they felt something was wrong, exactly the intended emotional response. Face tracking produced mixed reactions: some users were amused, others unsettled ("I don't like looking at him"). The experience worked 100% of the time in testing. Text size and copy were updated for readability and impact after feedback.

  • ✓ Successfully scared, error message communicated the right feeling
  • ✓ 100% success rate, "ensure face is visible" instructions were effective
  • Text size increased for readability after user feedback
  • Copy rewritten to be scarier, colours and tone adjusted accordingly

PROCESS & REFLECTION

What we learned

Project Management

The project was divided across three defined roles, Visual Design, Project Manager & Narrative, and Immersive Media Lead, ensuring clear ownership over every task. A Gantt chart tracked deliverables across the full timeline, mapping tasks to team members and deadlines from proposal through final delivery.

The narrative concept was locked early, with character arcs for both Damon and Enzo solidified before development began. Physical, digital, and immersive artifacts were developed in parallel, designed from the start to connect into one cohesive player journey.

Feedback loops were built into the process, usability testing findings directly drove design and copy revisions (lock icons, text size, AR copywriting) before final delivery.

User Testing Outcomes

Testing across all major components revealed a consistent pattern: the emotional and atmospheric goals were landing, but some interactive mechanics needed clearer affordances. The VR puzzle was the most critical failure point, users didn't realize the scrambled images could be rearranged, treating an interactive element as decoration.

The AR experience was the strongest performer, 100% success rate, genuinely scared users, and produced the intended unsettling emotional response. The Instagram arc was also validated: users accurately interpreted Damon's personality and character descent without any explicit guidance.

The journal's mysterious flavour text and glitchy aesthetic received consistent praise, confirming the visual and tonal direction was correct.

What Worked

The physical entry points, the angry note and the toolbox, created genuine moments of surprise and delight that set the right tone. The candy reward was small but meaningful. The dual rabbit holes on Instagram (post and comments) ensured no user hit a dead end transitioning to the VR.

AI-assisted development compressed timelines substantially. The journal went from concept to deployed live web app without anyone writing code from scratch, freeing the team's time for narrative and immersive work. Rapid iteration loops via Claude made post-testing design changes fast and low-effort.

The USB stick as a physical-to-digital bridge was a distinctive choice that reinforced the world, something you find at a real desk in a real classroom, connecting to a website that feels like it belongs to the same world.

What to Improve

The VR experience needs clearer puzzle affordances, users must understand the scrambled images are interactive before they give up. Camera speed caused dizziness and should be slowed. The floating hands need stronger visual menace to read as threatening rather than decorative.

QR code fatigue was noted across the class showcase environment. Future iterations might explore non-QR entry mechanisms to differentiate the experience from other projects in the same space.

AI tools were treated honestly, not every attempt worked. Claude's Spline troubleshooting attempt wasn't implemented. This is documented as a real outcome, not hidden. Understanding AI's limitations alongside its capabilities is part of what makes the collaboration meaningful.
